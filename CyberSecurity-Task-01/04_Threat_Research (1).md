# Part D: Cyber Threat Research

## 1. Phishing

**Definition:** Phishing is a deceptive attack where criminals send fraudulent messages — most commonly emails — that impersonate trusted sources such as banks, government agencies, or tech companies, with the aim of tricking the recipient into clicking a malicious link, entering login credentials on a fake site, or downloading malware.

**Real-world Example:** In 2020, a large-scale phishing campaign targeted employees of major corporations by sending emails that appeared to come from internal IT departments, claiming accounts would be suspended. Employees who clicked the link were taken to convincing fake login pages that captured their credentials.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Always check the actual sender email address, not just the display name
- Be suspicious of any message that creates urgency or asks you to log in through a link
- Use email security tools that flag suspicious messages before they reach your inbox
- Enable MFA so stolen credentials alone are not enough to access your account
- When in doubt, navigate directly to the website instead of clicking the link

## 2. Malware

**Definition:** Malware refers to any software intentionally written to cause harm — including viruses that replicate and spread, trojans that disguise themselves as legitimate software, spyware that records activity silently, and adware that hijacks browsers. It can arrive through email attachments, malicious downloads, or infected storage devices.

**Real-world Example:** A student downloads what appears to be free software for a college project from an unofficial site. Unknown to them, the installer includes a keylogger that records every password typed and sends it to the attacker in the background.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Only download software from official or verified sources
- Keep antivirus software installed, updated, and set to scan automatically
- Do not plug in unknown USB drives or storage devices
- Keep the operating system fully patched to close known vulnerabilities
- Be cautious of browser pop-ups asking you to install plugins or extensions

## 3. Ransomware

**Definition:** Ransomware is a category of malware that, once installed, encrypts files on the victim's device or network, making them completely inaccessible. The attacker then demands a ransom payment — typically in cryptocurrency — in exchange for the decryption key.

**Real-world Example:** The 2021 Colonial Pipeline attack saw ransomware shut down one of the largest fuel pipeline systems in the United States. The company paid approximately $4.4 million in ransom and had to halt operations, causing fuel shortages across the southeastern US.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Maintain regular, tested backups stored offline or in isolated cloud environments
- Apply security patches promptly, especially on internet-facing systems
- Train staff not to open unexpected email attachments or click unknown links
- Use endpoint detection tools capable of identifying ransomware behavior early
- Restrict administrative privileges so malware cannot spread easily across the network

## 4. Social Engineering

**Definition:** Social engineering is a manipulation technique that exploits human psychology rather than technical vulnerabilities. Attackers build trust, create urgency, or impersonate authority figures to convince their targets to hand over sensitive information or take actions that compromise security.

**Real-world Example:** An attacker calls a company's front desk pretending to be a senior IT manager, urgently requesting that the receptionist reset a password or provide access credentials for "emergency maintenance." The receptionist, not wanting to question authority, complies.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Always verify identity through a separate, trusted channel before complying with sensitive requests
- Train staff to be comfortable questioning and escalating suspicious requests regardless of apparent authority
- Establish clear protocols for how sensitive actions (like password resets) are approved
- Never share passwords, OTPs, or personal data over the phone or chat with unverified parties

## 5. Data Breach

**Definition:** A data breach occurs when sensitive or private information is accessed, copied, stolen, or exposed without authorization — whether through external hacking, misconfigured systems, or insider negligence. Breaches often expose large volumes of personal data including names, emails, passwords, and payment details.

**Real-world Example:** The 2017 Equifax data breach exposed the personal information of approximately 147 million people — including Social Security numbers, birth dates, and addresses — due to an unpatched vulnerability in their web application framework.

**Prevention Methods:**
- Encrypt sensitive data both in storage and during transmission
- Apply the principle of least privilege — only authorized personnel should access sensitive data
- Conduct regular security audits and penetration tests to identify weaknesses
- Keep all software and frameworks updated with security patches
- Monitor accounts for signs of unauthorized access using breach-alert services like Have I Been Pwned
