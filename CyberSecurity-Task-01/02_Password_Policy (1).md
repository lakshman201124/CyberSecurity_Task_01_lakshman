# Part B: Password Security Review

## What makes a strong password?

A strong password is one that is difficult for both humans and automated tools to guess or crack. It should be long — at least 12 to 16 characters — and should combine a mix of uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters. Importantly, it should not be based on dictionary words, personal information, or common patterns like "123456" or "password." The longer and more random it is, the harder it is to crack even with modern brute-force tools.

## Why should passwords be unique?

Using the same password across multiple accounts creates a single point of failure. If one website is breached and your credentials are leaked, attackers can use those same details to break into your other accounts — a method called credential stuffing. Keeping passwords unique ensures that a compromise on one platform stays isolated and doesn't cascade into a full account takeover.

## What is Multi-Factor Authentication (MFA)?

Multi-Factor Authentication is a login method that requires more than just a password to verify identity. It adds one or more additional steps — such as entering a one-time code sent to your phone, using an authenticator app, or scanning a fingerprint — alongside your password. Even if someone knows your password, they cannot access your account without this second factor, making MFA one of the most effective protections available.

## What are the risks of password reuse?

- A breach on any one of the sites you use exposes all accounts sharing that password
- Attackers use automated tools to test leaked credentials across major platforms within hours of a breach
- High-value targets like banking or email accounts become vulnerable through less-secure third-party services you may have forgotten about
- Identifying and fixing the exposure becomes significantly harder when the same password is used in many places

## Password Policy — Best Practices

1. Use a minimum of 14 characters — longer passphrases are both stronger and easier to remember
2. Mix uppercase letters, lowercase letters, numbers, and special characters throughout
3. Never use personal information like your name, date of birth, or pet's name
4. Use a completely different password for every account — no variations of the same base password
5. Use a trusted password manager to generate and securely store unique passwords
6. Enable MFA on every account that supports it, prioritizing email and banking first
7. Avoid writing passwords down in plaintext; if you must, keep them in a physically secure location
8. Change passwords immediately if you receive a notification of a breach or suspicious login
