# Part F: Security Reflection

Completing this personal security audit gave me a structured way to look at my own digital habits — something I had never done in a deliberate, systematic way before. On the device side, the results were reassuring: my system runs Windows 11 Pro, is fully updated, Microsoft Defender is active with a clean recent scan, and the firewall is enabled across all network profiles. Knowing these basics are in place provides a solid foundation, but the audit also made me realize that I had never consciously verified any of this — I had simply assumed everything was fine, which is itself a security risk.

On the account side, reviewing Google, GitHub, and Instagram confirmed that MFA is active, passwords are strong, and recovery emails are set on all three. That said, the audit prompted me to think beyond just these three accounts — there are likely older accounts on less-frequently used platforms where my security hygiene may not be as thorough. Going forward, I plan to do a broader account audit and either secure or delete accounts I no longer actively use, since dormant accounts can still be exploited if breached.

The broader takeaway from this exercise is that cybersecurity is not a one-time setup — it's an ongoing discipline. Threats evolve constantly, and habits that feel secure today may not be sufficient tomorrow. Staying informed, checking settings periodically, and treating every account as a potential entry point are mindsets that matter as much as any individual technical control.

Cybersecurity is critical in today's world because nearly every aspect of modern life — communication, finance, education, healthcare, and employment — is tied to digital systems. A single compromised account or infected device can cause cascading damage that goes far beyond the individual, affecting people and institutions connected to them. Building secure habits early, before something goes wrong, is far easier and less costly than trying to recover after a breach.
