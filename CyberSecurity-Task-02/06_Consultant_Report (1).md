# Cybersecurity Posture Improvement Report

**Prepared for:** College Administration
**Prepared by:** Lakshman Govindan, Security Analyst (Intern)
**Subject:** How Can We Improve Our Cybersecurity Posture?

---

## Executive Summary

A comprehensive review of the college's digital environment has identified several critical risk areas that require immediate attention. This report outlines the five highest-priority risks currently facing the institution, five concrete recommendations to address them, and the expected benefits of implementing these changes. The goal is not simply compliance — it is to build a genuinely resilient security posture that protects students, staff, and institutional data on an ongoing basis.

---

## Top 5 Risks

1. **Phishing attacks targeting staff and student email accounts** — the most common entry point for breaches in educational institutions, and one that bypasses technical controls by exploiting human behavior.

2. **Misconfigured cloud storage leading to accidental data exposure** — college data stored on Google Drive or OneDrive is frequently shared too broadly, often by accident, resulting in sensitive information being publicly accessible.

3. **Unpatched systems providing exploitable vulnerabilities** — delayed software updates leave known security gaps open, which attackers actively scan for and exploit using automated tools.

4. **Weak or reused passwords on the student portal and staff systems** — credential-based attacks are one of the most effective and commonly used attack methods, particularly when MFA is not in place.

5. **Lack of a formal incident response plan** — without a documented process for responding to a breach, the college risks chaotic, slow, and costly responses that worsen the impact of any incident.

---

## Top 5 Recommendations

1. **Deploy MFA on all staff and student accounts**, starting with email, the student portal, and administrative systems — this alone blocks the vast majority of credential-based attacks.

2. **Audit and restrict cloud storage permissions** — conduct an immediate audit of shared files and enforce a policy that limits external sharing to only what is explicitly approved.

3. **Establish an automated patch management schedule** — critical patches should be applied within 48 hours of release; routine patches within two weeks.

4. **Launch a mandatory phishing awareness programme** — quarterly training combined with simulated phishing tests to build a staff and student base that is harder to deceive.

5. **Develop and test an incident response plan** — document roles, response steps, communication protocols, and escalation paths, then run a tabletop exercise to identify gaps before a real incident occurs.

---

## Expected Benefits

- **Significant reduction in successful phishing attacks** through MFA and awareness training — the two most effective tools against the most common attack type
- **Fewer data exposure incidents** through cloud storage audits and enforced sharing policies
- **Faster vulnerability closure** through structured patch management, reducing the window of exposure from weeks to days
- **Faster, more controlled incident response** with less reputational and operational damage when breaches do occur
- **Stronger institutional trust** among students, parents, faculty, and regulatory bodies, demonstrating that the college takes data protection seriously

---

## Conclusion

Improving the college's cybersecurity posture does not require rebuilding from the ground up. It requires closing the most significant gaps in a prioritized, methodical way. The five recommendations above address the five highest-probability, highest-impact risks identified in this assessment, and together they would meaningfully raise the bar against the most common attack categories targeting educational institutions today.
